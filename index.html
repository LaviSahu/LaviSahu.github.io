Japan — a country that runs on thinking about the next person2023 · 3 minOsaka · Kyoto · Tokyo · Mt. Fuji

The train into Tokyo arrived at 9:14 and it was 9:14. Not roughly. The attendant on the platform bowed toward a carriage that hadn't fully emptied yet, to no one in particular. In a packed morning train, nobody spoke. I spend my working life trying to make systems behave like this, and here was a whole country that simply did.

You feel it first as service and slowly understand it as design. The konbini that has exactly what you need at 2am. The bins sorted into categories you didn't know existed. Queues that organize themselves without a rope or a sign. Every small choice seems to have asked the same question first: what happens to the next person who touches this?

Kyoto and Osaka are the range of it. Kyoto is restraint — temples, raked gravel, a tea house where a man has poured the same way for decades. Osaka is louder, a street of food stalls where the cook hands you something perfect off a roaring wok and barely looks up. The Japanese word is shokunin: mastery as devotion, not ambition. It is the most foreign idea I brought home and the one I think about most.

I won't pretend it's all warmth. The same precision that makes everything work can feel like pressure, and as an outsider you are cared for and held at a polite distance at the same time. The order has a cost, and the people inside it pay some of it.

Still, I came back to my planning desk with one sentence. Reliability is a culture before it is ever a metric. Japan had simply decided that consideration was worth building into the concrete, and then built it.

Thailand — the order you can't see, in a city that looks like chaos2019 · 2026 · 3 minBangkok

Bangkok at night is everything a planner is trained to fix. Wires knot overhead like a diagram nobody finished. Motorbikes thread gaps that shouldn't exist. A woman runs a food cart with one burner, a cleaver, and a memory, and the queue never stops. By every metric I'm paid to optimize, it should not work. It works better than half the systems I've designed.

If Japan is order imposed from the top, Bangkok is order that grows from the bottom. There's a phrase you hear — mai pen rai, never mind, it's fine — and it's not laziness. It's slack. The city carries enormous redundancy: a thousand vendors, no master plan, no central kitchen, and you eat better and cheaper than in any food court an MBA ever designed. That is emergence, and you can taste it.

Between the heat and the engines there are the temples — gold against the grey, a sudden quiet, people unhurried in a way I'm not. The warmth is real and it's everywhere, in the over-feeding, the directions you didn't ask for, the patience with a tired family finding its way.

I won't romanticize it. The same looseness that makes the city breathe also leaves people behind, and a hot afternoon stuck in traffic is not a metaphor for anything. But Bangkok taught me something my spreadsheets resist: not every good system is a designed system. Sometimes resilience is just slack you were too efficient to leave in.

I came home holding the controls a little less tightly.

Switzerland & Europe — what reliability looks like, and the strange timing of seeing it2020 · 4 minBasel · Zürich · Amsterdam · Luxembourg · Belgium

The Swiss train doors close with a sound like a decision. Basel runs the way you'd run a system if you optimized for one thing above all others: that it can be relied upon. The timetable is true. The water from the tap is a kind of moral statement. It is clean in a way that feels less like cleaning and more like a value.

I travel with a supply-chain brain, and Switzerland reads as trust turned into infrastructure. You don't verify the train, the institution, the price; you just rely on them, and that reliance is the whole product. It's expensive, and the expense is the point — you are paying for a guarantee.

What I didn't know, walking those quiet streets with my family, was the date. It was late February into March 2020. The news thickened a little every evening. Borders were about to close behind us; we just couldn't see it yet. There's a particular privilege, which I only understood later, in having one last open trip before the world stopped.

Honesty check: the same order can feel sterile. Reliability and warmth are not the same thing, and a society optimized for one can run a little short on the other. Some days Basel felt like a beautifully maintained machine I was visiting rather than a place that needed me there.

We flew home a few days before everything shut. The trip stopped being a holiday and became a marker — the last "before." I think about it whenever someone tells me a system is too reliable to be interesting. Reliability is what you miss the instant it's gone.

Singapore — a country run like a product2022 · 3 minMarina Bay · Gardens by the Bay · Sentosa · Johor Bahru

At 7:45 the Supertrees at Gardens by the Bay light up, and they light up on schedule. The MRT arrives clean and cool. The airport is somewhere you'd visit even without a flight. Singapore is the closest thing I've seen to a country designed the way I'm taught to design a supply chain: top-down, optimized, and executed with a discipline most companies never reach.

For an operations person it's a kind of utopia. Housing, transport, even water — they recycle it into something called NEWater and they're proud of it — all planned with a clarity that would make any boardroom jealous. Nothing is left to drift. The greenery itself is engineered, and somehow that doesn't make it less beautiful.

The MBA in me wants to call it proof. Here is central planning, run by people who actually execute, producing outcomes that the messier democracies I know can't match. It is the cleanest argument for planning I've ever walked through.

And then the honesty. The tradeoff is spontaneity. Mess, dissent, the unplanned — they've been engineered down, and you can feel the absence of them the way you feel air conditioning: comfortable, and a little sealed. A day trip across to Johor Bahru is a jolt — louder, looser, more alive in the way only an unoptimized place can be.

I admire Singapore the way I admire a perfectly tuned system. Completely, and with one quiet question I can't put down: what got optimized away to make it this smooth?

On strategy

No luck, only systems

I don't play games to escape systems thinking — I play to indulge it. Dota 2 is supply-and-demand under fire. Age of Empires is the S&OP loop I run at work, sped up and with trebuchets. The board games are quieter versions of the same itch. Add dice-luck and I lose interest fast.

On engines

The engine-builder's brain

A good Euro game is a constraint problem you actually want to solve. Castles of Burgundy is inventory placement; Splendor is tempo and compounding; Gizmos is a literal engine you build mid-game. Same muscle as network design at work — better decisions, better outcomes, no hiding behind a slide deck.
